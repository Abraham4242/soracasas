The build process, stage by stage

Updated 2026-06-24 · Owner: Abraham · INTERNAL build playbook (1 of 4)
↖ Overview→ Cost & budget → Materials & finishes by tier → Architecture & permits → Contractor & team

A custom home in Panama runs 10 to 12 months from signed contract to keys for a single-story; 12 to 15 for a two-story or a difficult site. Below is the full sequence, with realistic durations, what share of the budget each stage consumes, who is on the hook, the buyer's touchpoint, and the mistakes that blow up timelines. Phases overlap in reality (permits run while design finishes; MEP overlaps structure), which is why the calendar is shorter than the sum of the stages.

Read this with the money in mind. The percentages below are share of the construction cost. They map to the milestone-payment schedule (see Contractor & Team). Sora's promise is a fixed turnkey price locked at contract, with payments released only against verified progress at each stage.

Phase 0 · Land & feasibility (before any contract)

This happens before the build clock starts, but it is where builds are won or lost. Titled land only. Confirm titled vs Rights of Possession (ROP), pull a fresh Public Registry certificate, get a survey, and check Paz y Salvo (tax clearance). Verify utilities at the lindero (water, power, road), slope, and access. A quick soil read and a feasibility budget against the model and tier set the fixed price. If the lot can't be built on sanely, this is where we say "not yet."

  • Deliverable: verified title, survey, utilities confirmation, feasibility budget, signed fixed-price contract + spec annex.
  • Pitfall: ROP land marketed as "beachfront," missing utilities priced as an afterthought, slope that triples earthwork. Never deposit before title + survey.

The seven build stages

1

Design + plans

6–10 weeks7–10% of cost
What happens
  • Anteproyecto (concept) from the chosen model, adapted to the lot, orientation, breeze and views. The buyer reviews in 2D and in the 3D configurator/model.
  • Construction drawings: architectural, structural, electrical, plumbing/sanitary, all signed and sealed by JTIA-licensed (idóneo) professionals (see Architecture & permits).
  • Finish and material selections locked to the tier (Esencial / Sora / Firma), which freezes the BOM and the fixed price.
Who
Licensed architect (idóneo) + structural/electrical/sanitary engineers. Sora coordinates; buyer approves.
Buyer touchpoint
Concept review, one or two revision rounds, then sign-off on plans + finishes. After sign-off, changes become change orders.
Pitfall
Endless revisions. Lock the design. Every post-sign-off change costs time and money and is the #1 silent timeline killer.
2

Permits

8–14 weeks1–2% of cost
What happens
  • Plan review through MIVIOT's Ventanilla Única (which coordinates MINSA, IDAAN, Bomberos, the electric utility, MiAmbiente) + the municipal (alcaldía / DOYC) construction permit; ANATI involvement on titled coastal land.
  • Runs largely in parallel with the tail of design and the start of site prep, so it rarely costs the full 8–14 weeks on the critical path.
Who
The idóneo architect/engineer submits; Sora tracks. The buyer does nothing here except wait.
Pitfall
Coastal/ANATI files and rural municipalities move slowly and unpredictably. Build the slack in; never promise a ground-breaking date before permits are in hand.
3

Earthwork + foundation

4–6 weeks12–15% of cost
What happens
  • Soil study drives the foundation. Clearing, cut/fill, platform, then reinforced-concrete footings/piles and the slab (3,000 PSI concrete standard, Grade 60 rebar, REP-2021 seismic code).
  • Underground plumbing and electrical sleeves go in before the slab pour. Termite/humidity barriers on the coast and in Bocas.
Who
GC + foreman (maestro de obra), excavation sub, the structural engineer signing off the pour.
Buyer touchpoint
First big milestone photo/video set. Slab poured = visible, fundable progress.
Pitfall
Skipping or rushing the soil study; pouring on poor fill. Foundation problems are the most expensive to fix later. This is where cheap builders cut.
4

Structure + roof

12–16 weeks30–35% of cost
What happens
  • Walls go up: concrete block on the coast, M2/EMMEDUE panel common in the highlands. Columns, beams, ring beam, second-floor slab if two-story.
  • Roof structure + cover (standing-seam metal, flat TPO, clay, or treated iraca thatch by design), plus the building dries in (windows/doors openings framed).
Who
GC, masons, steel crew, roofers, the structural engineer.
Buyer touchpoint
The most satisfying stretch on video: the house takes its shape. Biggest single payment milestone.
Pitfall
The longest stage and where schedule slip accumulates. Weather (rainy season May–Nov), material lead times, and crew availability all bite here.
5

MEP rough-in (mechanical, electrical, plumbing)

6–8 weeks10–12% of cost
What happens
  • In-wall electrical (200A panel, THHN, Cat6/fiber), plumbing supply + drains, A/C line sets, and data before walls are closed and plastered.
  • Water tank + pressure pump, septic (3-chamber) or municipal tie-in, solar conduit if off-grid (Pedasí/Bocas).
Who
Electrical and sanitary subs under the GC; the MEP engineers sign off before close-up.
Pitfall
Closing walls before inspection. Anything wrong here is buried behind finishes, so verify rough-in before plaster.
6

Finishes

10–14 weeks25–30% of cost
What happens
  • Plaster + paint, floors (porcelain / engineered wood / marble / polished concrete by tier), kitchen + baths, countertops, doors, fixtures, A/C units, built-ins.
  • Pool, outdoor kitchen, landscaping, driveway, gates. This is where the tier (Esencial/Sora/Firma) becomes visible and where the home is sold emotionally.
Who
Finish trades: tilers, carpenters, painters, kitchen/cabinet installers, pool sub, landscaper.
Buyer touchpoint
High-frequency updates; finish selections confirmed against the spec annex (no surprises if locked at Stage 1).
Pitfall
Imported finishes (Italian marble, high-end appliances) with long lead times that weren't ordered early. Order long-lead Firma items during Stage 4.
7

Handover + occupancy

2–4 weeks0–2% of cost
What happens
  • Punch list (lista de pendientes) walked and cleared, final municipal (DOYC) inspection + Bomberos occupancy certificate, permiso de ocupación, utility connections activated.
  • Registering the improvement (mejoras) at the Public Registry for property tax; handing over warranties, manuals, as-built notes, and keys.
Who
GC clears punch list; Sora + buyer (or buyer's rep) do the final walk; municipality inspects.
Buyer touchpoint
Final walk-through (in person or by video), punch-list sign-off, then retainage released. Keys.
Pitfall
Releasing the final payment before the punch list is truly done. Hold retainage (5–10%) until it is.
