Panama QIV Visa & Real Estate

The $300,000 threshold closes October 15, 2026. After that, Panama's Qualified Investor Visa requires $500,000 in real estate. This guide covers exactly what qualifies, how SORA builds are structured to meet the requirement, and how the full application process works — including a comparison of QIV against Panama's other permanent residency pathways.

By SORA Real Estate Editorial · 16 min read · Updated July 3, 2026

Panama offers several paths to permanent residency, but the Qualified Investor Visa stands apart for one reason: it asks nothing of you except capital. No pension. No Panama employer. No years of provisional status. Invest $300,000 or more in qualifying real estate and you receive permanent residency immediately, with the right to live, work, and eventually naturalize in one of the most strategically located countries in the hemisphere. That $300,000 floor disappears on October 15, 2026, when it doubles to $500,000. For buyers already considering Panama property, that deadline is one of the clearest signals the market has sent in years.

This guide covers every material fact about the QIV for real estate buyers: what the visa gives you, what property qualifies, how SORA's build model is structured to meet the requirement, and what the application process looks like from signed contract to residency card in hand.

What the QIV actually gives you

The Panama Qualified Investor Visa was established by Executive Decree 722 and gives eligible foreign nationals immediate permanent residency — not temporary, not conditional, but permanent — upon approval. That's the first thing to understand: most immigration pathways in Panama involve a provisional period of one to two years before permanent residency is granted. The QIV skips that entirely.

What you receive:

  • Permanent residency from day one. Full carné (residency card) with no expiration, no renewal cycle, no re-application required.
  • Right to live and work. You can work for a Panama employer or run a Panama business without a separate work permit.
  • Family inclusion. Spouse and unmarried children under 18 (or under 25 if enrolled full-time in school) receive the same residency status on your application.
  • Citizenship pathway. After five years of permanent residency, you can apply for Panama citizenship and passport — one of the more useful travel documents in the region, with visa-free access to over 140 countries.
  • No physical presence requirement. You do not need to live in Panama. You must maintain the qualifying investment (keep the property), but you can spend as much or as little time in Panama as you choose.
  • Tax advantages. Panama operates on a territorial tax system — income earned outside Panama is not taxed in Panama, regardless of your residency status.

The QIV is in practice a plan-B domicile mechanism as much as a retirement tool. UK and EU holders use it as a second domicile and asset base. FIRE investors use it to establish a low-tax residence while maintaining global mobility. The visa's value is proportional to how much optionality matters to the holder.

The October 15 deadline, what's actually changing

Panama's Servicio Nacional de Migración confirmed the minimum qualifying investment for real estate under the QIV will rise from $300,000 to $500,000 for applications received on or after October 15, 2026. Applications submitted with complete documentation before that date — including proof of a qualifying investment of $300,000 or more — retain eligibility at the lower threshold.

There is no grace period. There is no transition window. October 15 is the hard cutoff.

What "before October 15" means in practice

The QIV application itself does not need to be approved before October 15 — it needs to be submitted with qualifying documentation before that date. Your attorney files with the SNM; the processing clock starts from submission. That said, document apostillation, background checks, and medical certificates typically take 6–10 weeks to assemble. If you're reading this in July and want to qualify at $300K, the timeline is tight but workable: contract signed, lot registered, attorney engaged by August means submission before mid-October is realistic.

After the threshold change, the QIV remains available — it just requires $500,000 instead of $300,000. For buyers with larger budgets or who are purchasing after the deadline, the visa is still one of the most accessible investor residency programs in Latin America. But for buyers already planning a Panama property purchase, locking in before October 15 saves $200,000 in required investment.

Real estate as the qualifying investment

The QIV accepts three types of qualifying investments. Real estate is both the most common and, at $300K, currently the most accessible:

For the real estate route, the investment amount is the registered purchase price or construction contract value — whichever is on record with the Public Registry of Panama. The property must be:

  • Titled — registered with the Public Registry as a fee-simple titled property. ROP (Rights of Possession) land does not qualify for the QIV. This is one of the most important distinctions and one that many buyers miss.
  • In Panama — obviously. All four SORA markets (Pedasí, Boquete, Volcán, Bocas del Toro) are fully eligible.
  • Held for five years minimum — the investment must be maintained for five years to preserve residency status. Selling before five years requires reinvestment into another qualifying asset.

The application process, step by step

The QIV is processed by Panama's Servicio Nacional de Migración (SNM). Here is the full sequence:

Step 1 — Engage a Panama immigration attorney (8–10 weeks before deadline)

Choose a licensed Panama attorney who handles QIV applications regularly. Fee: $3,500–$5,500 for full service. Do not try to self-file — the SNM process has specific document formatting and apostillation requirements that vary by country of origin, and attorneys know the current queue times.

Step 2 — Assemble your documents

  • Valid passport (apostilled if required by SNM for your country)
  • FBI background check or equivalent (apostilled) — allow 6–12 weeks for FBI, shorter for home-country equivalents
  • Bank statements showing liquidity for the investment ($300K+)
  • Medical certificate from a Panama-approved physician
  • Proof of qualifying investment: registered land title or purchase contract + SORA construction agreement
  • Passport photos
  • Marriage certificate if including spouse (apostilled)
  • Birth certificates for any dependent children (apostilled)

Step 3 — Attorney files with SNM

Your attorney submits the complete package to the SNM. You receive a receipt confirming the application date — this is the date that determines whether you qualify at the $300K or $500K threshold.

Step 4 — Provisional residency issued (30–60 days)

The SNM typically issues a provisional residency document within 30–60 days of submission. This is legally valid residency and allows you to obtain a provisional cédula (Panama ID number). You can open bank accounts, register vehicles, and conduct legal affairs in Panama.

Step 5 — Full permanent residency card (4–6 months total)

The full carné arrives 4–6 months from initial submission for most applicants. This is the permanent residency card with no expiration. At this point you are a permanent resident of Panama.

Which markets work at each threshold

If your goal is QIV qualification specifically, here is how Panama's major foreign-buyer markets stack up against both the current and post-October-15 thresholds:

Pedasí (Azuero coast)

The easiest market to hit $300K at a mid-size build. A 180 m² home at Sora tier runs $315K construction alone — add any lot and you're well over the threshold. For $500K, you're looking at 220–250 m² with a coastal lot, or a Firma-tier build on a premium beachfront parcel. Land in Pedasí ranges from $35K for a flat interior lot to $220K+ for oceanfront. The most common $500K QIV path in Pedasí: 200 m² Firma tier ($380K build) + $150K coastal lot = $530K total.

Boquete (Chiriquí highlands)

Boquete's higher land prices actually help QIV buyers hit the $500K threshold more easily than Volcán. A 220 m² home at Sora tier ($360K) plus a valley-view lot ($120K+) = $480K+. With a slightly larger build or premium lot, $500K is straightforward. Boquete is the preferred market for UK and EU QIV buyers who want a year-round temperate climate rather than a seasonal beach property.

Volcán (higher Chiriquí)

Panama's lowest-cost highland market. SORA builds at Esencial tier start at $890/m² — meaning $300K is achievable at smaller builds than anywhere else. For $500K, buyers typically go larger (280+ m²) or choose a premium finca parcel with mountain views. Volcán land prices are the most affordable in the highland markets: $25K–$100K for a titled lot in most cases.

Bocas del Toro (archipelago)

The most expensive build market ($1,150–$1,500/m² at SORA tier) because of island logistics and salt-air construction requirements. A 200 m² build in Bocas starts at $330K construction and exceeds $500K total when combined with an island lot ($80K–$250K). Bocas is the easiest market to hit both thresholds but also the most expensive to build in. For buyers who want the Caribbean island lifestyle, the QIV math works at almost any SORA build size.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use a build contract — not a completed home — to qualify for the QIV?

Yes. A signed, registered purchase contract for titled land plus a fixed-price construction agreement qualifies as the investment. You do not need the home to be completed before applying. SORA's fixed-price contracts are structured to document the qualifying investment amount clearly for immigration attorneys. Many SORA clients begin their immigration process while construction is underway.

What is the exact October 15 deadline?

Applications submitted with complete qualifying documentation before October 15, 2026 can qualify at the $300,000 threshold. The application does not need to be approved before that date — it needs to be submitted. There is no grace period once October 15 passes. Given that background checks and apostillations take 6–10 weeks, buyers who want to qualify at $300K should begin the document process in July or August at the latest.

Does the QIV require me to live in Panama?

No. The QIV does not require physical presence in Panama. You must maintain the qualifying investment (the titled real estate) for a minimum of five years to preserve your residency status, but there is no minimum days-per-year requirement. Many QIV holders live primarily elsewhere and use Panama as a legal domicile, banking base, and backup residency.

Can my spouse and children be included on my QIV?

Yes. Your spouse and unmarried children under 18 (or under 25 if enrolled full-time in school) receive the same permanent residency on your application. Each dependent adds a modest fee to the application total but does not change the investment requirement. Dependents can also work in Panama without a separate work permit once the residency is issued.

What happens to my QIV if I sell the qualifying property?

If you sell the qualifying real estate before five years, you must reinvest the proceeds into another qualifying asset (real estate, government bonds, or exchange-listed securities) to maintain residency. Your immigration attorney should structure and document this reinvestment. Failure to maintain a qualifying investment could result in loss of residency, though Panama's SNM typically allows a cure period to reinvest.

How does the QIV differ from the Friendly Nations Visa?

The QIV is purely investment-based — no employment, business registration, or Panama economic ties required. The FNV requires demonstrated economic ties to Panama, typically through employment with a Panama company or ownership of a Panama-registered corporation with active operations. For buyers who don't have existing Panama business operations, the QIV is usually simpler to qualify for. The FNV has no minimum investment, but the QIV requires $300K (rising to $500K after Oct 15).

How long does the full QIV process take?

From complete document submission to provisional residency: 30–60 days. From submission to full permanent residency card: 4–6 months. Your attorney can obtain a provisional cédula (Panama ID number) shortly after submission that serves as valid residency proof while the full processing completes. You can open bank accounts and conduct legal affairs in Panama with the provisional document.
