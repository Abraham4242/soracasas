The contractor & build team

This is the highest-risk decision in the whole project. Panama has light construction-inspection enforcement, slow courts (civil cases run 2–7 years), and a recurring failure mode where an absentee foreign owner pays a big deposit and the builder underperforms or vanishes. Sora's edge is to be the buyer's professional gatekeeper: source on track record (never lowest bid), vet against documents (not promises), contract fixed-price through the buyer's own attorney, pay only against verified progress, and represent the buyer on the ground.
Two corrections to common assumptions: (1) the body that licenses architects/engineers is JTIA (state regulator) — vet for JTIA idoneidad; SPIA is the guild, not the license. (2) "Attorney-held escrow" is a misnomer in Panama — regulated escrow is bank/trust-company only (Law 1/1984, Law 21/2017); an attorney structures/oversees it but funds sit in a licensed fiduciary or law-firm trust account.

Sourcing by region

Universal method: generate leads from regional expat networks, then qualify only by (a) physically visiting completed projects and (b) calling past foreign clients with the builder absent. Never choose on lowest bid — "under-quote to win, then inflate via change orders" is a named Panama fraud pattern.

  • Boquete / David / Chiriquí: the deepest pool of callable references and finished homes (largest expat-build market). FB: "Expats in Boquete," "ExPats in Panama."
  • Pedasí / Azuero: thin GC market — searches surface agencies, not builders, so source the GC via agents + developer relationships. Verify a registered finca before engaging.
  • Volcán: smallest market; lean on the Chiriquí-wide pool (shares David's). Cool climate removes A/C; confirm well/stream water on rural lots.
  • Bocas: logistics dominate — every material barged in (~+25–30%), abandonment risk highest here. FB: "Bocas Del Toro Expats," "Bocas Foro Comunitario," The Bocas Breeze directory.

Paid relocation services sell "vetted" contractor lists — treat as paid leads, not endorsements. Always use a local idóneo architect to redraw/stamp imported plans. Best build start: dry season (Dec/Jan).

Vetting checklist (clear every applicable item before signing)

Legal & licensing

  • Aviso de Operación — verify on PanamaEmprende (a solo licensed professional is exempt; a construction company must have one).
  • Registro Público — Certificado de Vigencia: company Active, tasa única paid, confirm directors/resident agent.
  • JTIA idoneidad of the architect/engineer who signs & seals the plans — verify on JTIA "Consulta de Profesionales Idóneos"; request a formal certificate for the file.
  • Confirm the company itself is JTIA-registered for construction works.
  • SPIA membership = a positive signal, not a substitute for JTIA idoneidad.

Labor & insurance compliance

  • CSS employer registration (every worker affiliated within 6 business days, Law 51/2005).
  • CSS paz y salvo (current clearance) — a missing one signals the builder is behind on social security.
  • Riesgos Profesionales (workers' comp, employer-funded, high-rated for construction) paid.
  • CAR all-risk construction insurance active for this project (MAPFRE / Acerta / Nacional).
  • Contract clause making the builder solely responsible for all CSS/labor payments + indemnifying the owner.

Permits

  • Permiso de Construcción path clear — plans pre-approved by DOYC; application signed by both owner and the JTIA professional.
  • Plan for the Permiso de Ocupación — must be requested by the same party who pulled the construction permit (keeps the builder bound through completion).

Track record & financial

  • Independently sourced project list (not a curated hand-out).
  • Physically inspect ≥3 finished homes of similar scope.
  • Call past foreign clients privately, builder absent — ask about delays, change-order behavior, post-handover service; look for patterns.
  • Financial stability — bank reference letter, supplier references, APC credit history with consent.
  • Litigation/complaints check (Órgano Judicial, Panadata, ACODECO).
  • Performance bond (fianza de cumplimiento) — negotiate as a term for larger builds (not standard in residential).
  • Any foreign "contractor" isn't operating illegally (some roles reserved for Panamanians).
  • No red-flag behavior present (see below).

The contract

  • Fixed-price (precio alzado) is the protective default — price is fixed and invariable; the contractor bears its own overrun risk. Cost-plus shifts overrun risk to the buyer and is hard to police remotely.
  • Use the buyer's OWN independent Panama attorney — never the builder's, developer's, agent's, or "the bank's standard" form. No one helping you build owes you a fiduciary duty; the buyer's attorney + Registry due diligence is the primary safeguard.
  • Specs annex: integrate the memoria descriptiva, técnica specs, plans, metrados, itemized budget, and a valued progress calendar as binding annexes — the anchor for milestone verification.
  • Change orders: every change a written, numbered instrument (scope, cost, time impact), signed by both parties before work proceeds — the main defense against cost inflation.
  • Delay penalty (cláusula penal, Civil Code Arts. 1039–1042): a fixed sum per day/week late, enforceable without proving damage. Note: by default the penalty substitutes for damages (stipulate "plus damages" if wanted) and a judge may reduce a disproportionate one.
  • Warranty — the strongest protection (Civil Code Art. 1343): the contractor is liable 10 years for structural ruin from construction defects (15 years where the cause is breach of contract terms); the directing architect/engineer is liable on the same terms. Courts read "ruina" broadly (functional unfitness). A builder's shorter warranty cannot override this statutory decennial liability for housing. Also negotiate a 1-year punch-list warranty on top.
  • Dispute resolution: specify arbitration at CeCAP (Ley 131/2013) — courts are slow (2–7 yr). Construction + real estate are CeCAP's largest caseloads.

Payment structure

RuleDetail
DepositModest — ≤10% at signing (developer condos run ~33%, but that's not a bespoke build)
Milestone drawsTied to defined, inspected stages; cumulative paid must never exceed inspected work-in-place
Retention5–10% of each draw withheld to final acceptance + occupancy permit
EscrowRoute through a licensed trust company (or a law-firm trust account — legally not the same) with an independent attorney overseeing release. Never to the builder's personal/offshore account.

Anti-front-loading is the whole game: deposit ≤10%, every payment against an inspected milestone, cumulative paid ≤ work-in-place, retention held to the end, funds via fiduciary not personal account. ≥30–40% upfront, or any cash-only / personal-account demand, is a deal-breaker.

Red flags that end the conversation

  • No written, bilingual, attorney-reviewed contract — or the builder insists on his own form.
  • Large upfront deposit (≥30–40%) or near-full payment before substantial work.
  • Cash-only, or wire to a personal/offshore account (also exposes the buyer to tax-fraud liability).
  • No JTIA-idóneo professional signing the plans; no JTIA-registered company.
  • No CSS registration / paz y salvo / riesgos profesionales / CAR insurance.
  • No permits pulled (Construcción + the continuity-critical Ocupación).
  • No callable references, or references you can't speak to privately.
  • Lowest bid by a wide margin (classic under-quote-then-pad).
  • Builder rarely on site / relies on "changes and additions" to inflate cost.
  • Pre-construction developer who can't prove land title or infrastructure funding (highest abandonment risk, esp. Bocas).

Managing a build remotely (the Sora operating standard)

  • The JTIA-licensed professional is the legal supervisor but is typically aligned with the build — so the buyer needs separate representation. An independent owner's representative / project manager is the universally recommended fix and is not a regulated Panama profession — Sora filling this role IS the product.
  • Weekly dated photo + video walk-throughs tied to the milestone schedule.
  • Independent third-party inspector (a separate licensed civil engineer/architect, not the builder's) certifies avance de obra before each milestone payment — government inspection "exists on paper but you can't count on it."
  • No draw released without inspector certification + owner-rep confirmation; owner-rep co-signs every change order.
  • Hold the 5–10% retention and keep the builder bound through the Permiso de Ocupación (same-party rule).
  • Set client expectations honestly: purely absentee builds "take twice as long and cost about twice as much"; best case is still 12–18 months. The remote-management apparatus is not polish — it is what makes a remote build survivable.

Labor reality (for fixed-price bid sanity)

  • Crew: concrete-block-and-steel standard; the GC coordinates masonry, electrical, plumbing, finishing, roofing cuadrillas. The maestro de obra (foreman) interprets plans, schedules crews, controls materials/quality — operational, not legal, responsibility (a regulated trade requiring its own idoneidad).
  • Wages (CAPAC-SUNTRACS, 2022–2025 agreement — likely expired in 2026, check for successor): peón ~$4.23–4.51/hr, skilled obrero calificado ~$5.70–6.10/hr; monthly roughly $918 (peón) to $1,269 (calificado).
  • Employer on-costs ("loaded" labor): CSS employer contribution rising under Ley 462/2025 to 13.25% now → 14.25% (2027) → 15.25% (2029), + 1.50% seguro educativo, + riesgos profesionales ~4–6.25% (construction), + 13th-month/vacation → ~130–138% of gross.
  • Build-cost context (to sanity-check GC bids): ~$700–800/m² (~$65/sq ft) mid-range; national range ~$370–$2,200/m². The dominant variable is finishes (largely imported, up to ~5× US). Land is separate (~$40–50/m² avg).
Caveats: JTIA (not SPIA) licenses; "escrow" = licensed trust company, not literally the attorney's account; the CAPAC-SUNTRACS wage table has likely expired (check successor); retention/deposit %s are negotiated norms, not statute; most red-flag material is anecdotal (the patterns are reliable, specific %s indicative). Every legal step should be confirmed by a Panama-licensed attorney before a live deal.
